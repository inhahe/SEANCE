Pin Kinds (What the Cable Colors Mean)

Home Graph Basics

SoundShop2 has four kinds of pins, each carrying a different kind of data between nodes. Each kind has a distinct color so you can see at a glance what kind of data is flowing in a given cable. Note that "signal" below is the name of one of these four kinds — not a catch-all word for every kind of data flowing between nodes. Audio and MIDI are their own things, not kinds of signal.

Audio — Cornflower blue

Stereo audio signal. Each sample is a number roughly in the range -1.0 .. +1.0. Audio pins carry actual sound — what you hear at the Master Out. Synths produce audio, effects transform it, and the Master Out sends it to your speakers.

MIDI — Lime green

MIDI events: notes (on/off with velocity), pitch bend, aftertouch, control change (CC) messages, program changes. MIDI cables carry what to play, not the sound itself. Timelines emit MIDI from their clips; Computer Keyboard and hardware MIDI inputs emit live MIDI; instruments consume MIDI and turn it into audio.

Param — Orange (block-rate control)

A slow-changing control signal used to modulate parameters on other nodes. "Slow" here means it updates once per audio block (every ~64 samples, roughly 700 times per second at 44.1kHz) rather than once per sample. That's still way faster than you can perceive, but it's faster than automation drawn in a piano roll and slower than audio.

Param is what you want when you're driving a knob with an LFO, an envelope follower, an XY pad, or a MIDI CC mapping. It's the orange color and lives in the 700Hz range — perfect for the "move this knob smoothly" use case.

Signal — Amber (audio-rate control)

Also a control signal, but at audio rate — the same resolution as audio itself. A Signal pin carries a value that can change on every single sample, which is what you need for effects like:

  • Audio-rate FM (modulating pitch with an audio signal)
  • Audio-rate filter modulation (ring-modulated filter cutoff)
  • Sample-accurate envelope followers driving compression
  • Any modulation whose sound depends on the modulator being fast and smooth

Signal is overkill for most modulation and costs more CPU, but there are things you can only do with it. Think of it as "Param's sample-accurate cousin." It's colored amber — close to Param's orange on purpose, because they're both control signals, just at different rates.

Which should I use?

Rule of thumb: if you're modulating a knob and you can't hear an audible "zipper" when you move it quickly, Param is fine. Use Signal when your modulator is itself an oscillator whose waveform matters (FM, ring mod), or when you're doing something where the modulator's timing needs to line up sample-by-sample with audio (transient-aware dynamics, sync'd gating).

Cross-kind connections

You can only connect pins of matching kinds. Audio doesn't plug into MIDI, MIDI doesn't plug into Param, etc. If you try, SoundShop2 won't make the cable.

There are nodes that act as adapters between kinds — for example, the MIDI Modulator node takes a Signal input and a MIDI stream, and uses the signal to modify MIDI velocities / bend / CCs. That's how you "connect" a signal source to a MIDI flow.
